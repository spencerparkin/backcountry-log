Big Drop 1

So John will go and ski Big-Drop-1 by himself, probably because he knows he can get up there and back down in record-breaking time. And he can do 20 laps on the thing in world-record time. So why bring me along to slow him down? In any case, if he did want a partner, I'm sure I'd be last on the list. (Or not on the list at all.)

Anyhow, I had heard that he recently went up there and skied the damn thing, and I guess I felt like I should go try to ski it too before the next storm. (Just because a slope gets skied safely, that doesn't mean it's safe to ski!) I felt conditions were safe enough, so I decided to copy-cat a great skier (John.)

As can be seen in the video, whatever conditions John got, mine were certainly, let's say, less good. I should have known better. Much better snow could have been found on other aspects.

To make matters worse, it took me over an hour to reascend the ridge, because the snow was so wet that my skins wouldn't work. I had to boot it back up through the trees and use my poles to keep boyant in the snow. It was terribly exhausting, and a bit scary. I was worried that a wet-slide would bury me. The whole decision to ski this slope was just super, duper, dumb.

This same exact day, Owen and his friend (I forget his name) was up on B-ridge skiing sensable snow. Owen said he saw my skin track and looked for me, but didn't see me. I'm sure they got in, did a few laps, and got out, all in the time it took me to go down BD1 once, and then make my painful exit.

I had not yet met Owen in person at the time, so it was disappointing that I didn't meet up with him that day. I later met him in person at the Hidden Lake Circle trail-head for Dude Peak. We had a fun conversation. We're both very enthusiastic about the mountains. As far as getting together to ski, he told me not to take personally the fact that he never commits to a time and place to meet-up. He said that over the years he's learned he has to be last-minute spontaneous due to the time-constraints of being a family man working full-time. I certainly understand that, but apparently he's able to go last-minute with all his other ski partners.

The only other big-drop run I've done is BD12. It's a long way to BD12, and a long way back, but it's a beautiful, big open face with an exciting slope angle. I'm not sure how much of it I skied; I was scared of it, and skied off to the side after going down a few hundred feet or so. While ascending back up along a ridge, I accidentally broke a cornice and almost fell down it. It made a huge crashing noise, and created a bit of a slide. One of the lessons here is that if you're not paying attention, the ridge configuration can change on you quickly. In this case, the apex of the ridge was fine to travel on at the start, but turned into a corniced section before I realized it. Had I realized the change in ridge configuration, I would have dropped off the ridge onto the safe side, and then regained it further up.
