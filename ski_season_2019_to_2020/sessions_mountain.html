Session's Mountain

When viewing the Session's Mountains range, one potential ski line stands out above all others, and it's the one coming down the highest point along this "range" which is a sub-ridge of the Wasatch Range back-bone. It falls to the south-west (from what I'm just going to call Session's Peak) down into a drainage called Rocky Basin up top, and Willey Hollow nearing the bottom where it converges with Mueller Park Canyon.

Not only is the line aesthetically pleasing in my mind from a distance, but it is also a beautiful place when you're emerced in it. The aspect isn't ideal, but the area and line itself, I think, are really neat.

This is one of the great things, I think, about backcountry skiing. You see a line off in the distance, dream about skiing it, and then you go and make it happen. The video above actually shows my 2nd descent down this particular line. I did not have a GoPro camera for my 1st descent, which was under better conditions, but unfortunately, however, I wasn't as good of a skier at the time. I'd like to believe that I have improved since then.

As can be seen from the video, conditions this 2nd time around where pretty bad. It really didn't matter if I had gotten there later, because as was forcasted for this particular day, the sun was never going to be strong enough to soften the snow. Though a solar aspect, this particular line needs a bit more time than most to soften up, because it is shaded by the sub-ridge on skier's left for most of the morning. This is actually a good thing, because it gives you more time to arrive at the peak, which is already difficult to reach.

The last half mile or so along the ridge to the summit is the most difficult, and there are a few tricky and exposed sections. I'm thinking that next time I try this peak, I will drop into Holbrook Canyon, then skin up to the GWT (Great Western Trail), from which it is an easy-breezy hike up to Session's Peak.

I approached this time by going up the gas pipeline in Meuller Park canyon. Then exited via the Kenny Creek trail. This works out nicely, because the pipeline is a quick open approach without any tree-line difficulty in reaching the top of the mountain, and because you can ski a good portion of the Kenny Creek trail to and from the old broken-down cabin.

On my first attempt, I approached via the Holbrook Canyon trail-head going up by the old Christmas Tree farm to gain the ridge. I went back the same way, but lost the trail and ended up bushwhacking to the bottom of Holbrook Canyon, and happy to find the trail at the bottom. I recall fording all the streams on the way out, too tired to try or care to keep my feet dry.

The ski line featured in the video is over 30-degrees. I don't think I've measured it (I should have), but I'm sure it's well over 30. So it's a line I'd be too afraid to try mid-winter by myself. I've only tried it as a Spring ski when I'm confident we've had several freeze-thaw cycles to weld the snow into place. On the day of the attempt, it's then just a matter of skiing the face late enough that the snow softens, but not too late that it's become destabilized by the heat of the sun.
